## Definition

**Basal cognition** is the continuum view that intelligence and mind are not binary properties, but exist at all scales of biological organization — from molecules to cells to tissues to organisms.

It rejects the claim that "mind" suddenly emerges at some threshold (neurons, brains, humans). Instead, it proposes that goal-directed behavior, information processing, and decision-making exist at every level of life.

## Core Arguments Against the Binary View

Levin emphasizes that nature doesn't work in discrete categories with sharp transitions:

- **Developmental biology:** We are single cells that gradually become multicellular organisms. There's no moment where something "becomes" minded. The process is continuous.
- **Evolution:** Species evolve gradually. There's no generation where a species suddenly "gains" mind. If we accept this, mind must also be on a continuum.
- **Physics:** Nature doesn't create sharp boundaries (almost never). Water gradually freezes; salt gradually dissolves. Why would mind be different?

**The question isn't:** "Does X have a mind?" (binary)

**The question should be:** "What kind of mind and how much?" (continuous, quantified)

## Scaling Principles

### Cellular Level (Basal)
- Individual cells pursue **homeostatic goals** — maintaining internal chemical/electrical balance
- These goals are not conscious deliberation, but they are goal-directed behavior
- Cells make decisions: responding to stimuli, adjusting metabolism, initiating division or death
- This is minimal cognition but it's still cognition

### Multicellular Level (Intermediate)
- Cell collectives can pursue larger goals through **cognitive glue** mechanisms (gap junctions, electrical coupling)
- The collective's cognitive lightcone expands — larger goals become possible
- No central brain required; emergent behavior arises from local cell-cell communication

### Organismal Level (Advanced)
- Nervous systems create specialized information-processing tissues
- This allows even larger cognitive lightcones
- But it's not a new kind of cognition; it's a scaling-up of basal cognition

### Beyond Biology
- AI systems may not have neurons, but if they can pursue large, complex, abstract goals, they exhibit cognition
- The substrate is less important than the structure of goal-pursuit

## Why This Matters

**Philosophical Impact:**
- Solves some classical problems (hard problem of consciousness, mind-body problem) if consciousness is a continuous property rather than a binary state
- Redirects the search: instead of "where does consciousness come from?" ask "what conditions allow larger/more coherent goal-pursuit?"

**Medical Impact:**
- If cells have "native intelligence," then regenerative medicine is about *communicating* with that intelligence, not just manipulating molecules
- Bioelectric interfaces become communication channels, not puppet-strings

**AI Impact:**
- Intelligence isn't substrate-specific. Silicon-based, carbon-based, or hybrid systems can all be intelligent if they have the right organizational structure
- This opens new architectures beyond neural networks

## Evidence

1. **Single-celled organisms** solve navigation and resource-allocation problems without neurons
2. **Slime molds** solve maze problems and find optimal food distribution
3. **Plant roots** sense and respond to multiple environmental cues, anticipating future conditions
4. **Cell collectives** (xenobots, anthrobots) learn and coordinate without a brain
5. **Cancer cells** exhibit goal-directed behavior (spreading, resource competition) — they're using the same basal cognition we'd expect

## The Continuum (Simplified)

```
Atoms → Molecules → Cells → Tissues → Organisms → Communities → Civilizations
(no goal) (chemistry) (homeostasis) (local) (whole-body) (collective) (global)

All levels involve information processing and goal-directed behavior at some scale.
What changes is the SIZE of the goals, not the presence of cognition.
```

## Cross-Domain Connections

**Philosophy:** This is a form of **panpsychism lite** — not saying atoms are conscious, but saying intelligence/mind is fundamental to biological organization at all scales. Related to process philosophy (Whitehead) and neoplatonism.

**Neuroscience:** Challenges the neural essentialism that dominates cognitive science. Forces a reconceptualization of consciousness not as unique to brains, but as a scaling property of integrated systems.

**AI Architecture:** If basal cognition is about scaling goal-pursuit, then new AI architectures might be inspired by multi-scale biological organization (not just neural networks). Hierarchical goal-systems, decentralized decision-making, etc.

**Ethics:** If cells and cell collectives have basal cognition, then do we have ethical obligations toward them? Does a regenerating tissue have moral status? This becomes urgent with synthetic organisms.

## Open Questions

- **Lower bound:** At what scale does cognition start? Molecules? Organelles? Just cells?
- **Consciousness:** Is basal cognition the same as consciousness? Or is consciousness a special case of cognition (high-level, reflective)?
- **Measurement:** How do we empirically measure "how much" cognition a system has?
- **Suffering:** If trees are basal-cognitive, can they suffer? Do we have obligations?
