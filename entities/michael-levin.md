## Overview

Michael Levin is a professor of biology at Tufts University working at the intersection of biology, computer science, and philosophy. His research focuses on how cells use electrical signals to coordinate and decide what to build, with practical applications in regenerative medicine and synthetic biology.

## Research Focus

**Core claim:** Mind is not confined to neurons or brains, but is a property of biological systems at all scales. What we call "intelligence" exists on a continuum from single cells to organisms, and the mechanisms are fundamentally about information processing and goal-directed behavior — not substrate-specific.

**Key research areas:**
- **Bioelectricity** — How voltage gradients in non-neural tissues encode morphogenetic information and enable collective decision-making
- **Xenobots and Anthrobots** — Synthetic living machines created from frog cells, demonstrating that collections of cells can build novel structures and behaviors not programmed in DNA
- **Morphogenesis** — The mechanisms by which cell collectives achieve and maintain form, particularly the role of bioelectric pattern spaces
- **Cognitive lightcone** — A framework for understanding how the scale of goals (and thus agency/mind) scales from single cells to organisms

## Current Work

**At Tufts:** About 75% wet lab work on applications in regenerative medicine:
- Birth defects repair
- Organ regeneration
- Cancer reprogramming
- Aging interventions
- Degenerative disease reversal

**Key technology:** Bioelectric communication interfaces that allow researchers to "talk to" cell collectives and respcecify their goals without micromanaging molecular details.

**Xenobot/Anthropot research:**
- Demonstrated learning and memory formation in xenobots
- Kinematic self-replication (xenobots collect loose cells and organize them into new xenobots)
- Gene expression changes in xenobots not seen in parent embryos (including hearing-related genes)
- No strong heredity yet but reproducible morphology across generations

## Intellectual Position

Levin positions himself against several mainstream assumptions:
1. **Mind-brain identity:** Rejects the equation of mind with brain/neurons
2. **Sharp categories:** Argues mind/intelligence exist on a continuum, not binary states
3. **Genetic determinism:** DNA is necessary but not sufficient to explain morphogenesis; bioelectric patterns are crucial
4. **Reductionist mechanism:** Holds that "emergent" explanations obscure rather than explain — argues for Platonic space (mathematical patterns with causal efficacy)

## Open Questions He Emphasizes

- What is the full computational capacity of cell collectives?
- Where exactly are morphogenetic templates stored and retrieved?
- How do bioelectric patterns link to actual goal-directed behavior?
- Can we reliably interface with and redirect the "native intelligence" of tissues?
