## What They Are

**Xenobots:** Synthetic living machines created by reassembling cells from African clawed frog (*Xenopus*) embryos into novel morphologies and configurations not found in nature. They are not robots in the traditional sense — they are living tissue organized in new ways.

**Anthrobots:** Similar synthetic organisms created from biopsied human cells. The same principle: human cells capable of building entirely novel structures and behaviors outside their normal developmental context.

## Demonstrated Capabilities

### Learning and Memory
- Xenobots demonstrate **learning** — they form memories of events in their environment and retain them
- These memories can be read out experimentally
- This is demonstrated cognition at the level of individual xenobots

### Self-Replication
- **Kinematic self-replication:** Xenobots collect loose epithelial cells scattered in their environment
- They assemble these cells into structured piles
- Because the cells themselves have computational/bioelectric properties, these piles become the next generation of xenobots
- The new generation continues this process (Vonnegut's self-replicating robot dream)
- Note: Heredity not yet strongly demonstrated; form is reproducible but not yet clearly transgenerational

### Novel Gene Expression
- Xenobots upregulate several hundred genes not normally expressed in frog embryos
- Notably: clusters of genes related to sound perception/hearing
- When exposed to sound waves, xenobots respond to specific frequencies; embryos do not
- This demonstrates that the new morphology enables entirely new sensory capabilities

### Open Behavioral Questions
Levin explicitly states these are *still being tested*:
- Specific goal states
- Degree of intentional intelligence vs reactive behavior
- Preferences and decision-making
- Learning beyond memory formation
- Any form of true agency or volition

## Why This Matters

Xenobots/anthrobots demonstrate that:

1. **Morphology is not deterministic from DNA** — cells can self-organize into novel forms that aren't "programmed" in their genetic instructions
2. **Living systems are programmable** — cell collectives respond to bioelectric signals and can be "redirected" toward different goals
3. **The substrate of intelligence might be generalizable** — if frog cells can learn and organize in novel ways, what constraints limit possible forms of intelligence?
4. **Ethics becomes urgent** — if we can create synthetic organisms with unknown cognitive capacities, we need frameworks for understanding their moral status

## Cross-Domain Connections

**AI / Machine Learning:** Xenobots are a proof that biological substrates can solve problems without explicit algorithms or neural networks. This challenges the assumption that intelligence requires neurons. Related to concepts of embodied cognition and morphological computation.

**Materials Science:** The ability to reprogram cell collectives to build specific structures suggests a future of "grown" materials and programmable matter — far beyond current 3D printing.

**Philosophy of Mind:** Xenobots are living counterexamples to the equation "mind = brain." They exhibit learning and goal-directed behavior with no central nervous system. This resurrects questions about the location and substrate of consciousness.

**Medicine:** If we can reprogram human cells (anthrobots) to solve problems, could we use this same principle to fix birth defects, regenerate organs, or reprogram cancer cells?

## Open Questions

- Can xenobots *learn* to reprogram themselves, or can only external bioelectric signals change their goals?
- What is the upper limit of their cognitive lightcone — how complex can their goals become?
- Do they have preferences? Desires? Any form of subjective experience?
- How do we ethically treat organisms we create but don't fully understand?
